My Experience @ Dublin High School:

When it comes to learning, nothing beats hands-on work. Experiencing workflow/work through actual field work is leaps and bounds more useful than just sitting in front of a textbook. Of course, aside from the field work that can be seen in my about me section and on my resume, I have been fortunate enough to be part of a program which also pushes its students to gain field experience. Here are some of my first-hand experiences in the field with my fellow classmates.

First off, there were field trips. One trip that our class took was to SAP. At SAP, us students were given a tour of the facility along with an in depth description of what the company does. Many of the staff members explained their positions and roles and got us curious. I had a couple important takeaways after going to be SAP. First off, extracurriculars are always good. Working outside of school, in any way possible, is always a benefit. Second, networking skills are key. You don't necessarily have to change yourself as a person, but you must be able to be open and communicate. Another interesting field trip was to Workday, in which we had a luncheon with some engineers from different teams at the company. There was a lot of great career advice on how to go through high school and college and hopefully end up at the college you want.

Secondly, there are guest speakers. Civil engineers and guest speakers Dan Cherrier and Natalina Bernardi spoke to our Princples of Engineering class on civil engineering as a major in college and as a career. While giving they gave the presentation, I found out about the different licenses and requirements for being a civil engineer. Some things that piqued my interest were when both presenters said that one, you can get a high position from just a bachelor's degree, and two, civil engineering is a very difficult. I didn't expect this, as I always thought it wasn't that difficult of a major, but the more you know the more interesting things become. Another interesting guest speaker was Jason Yoskinski, from Uber AI Labs. He gave a presentation on the basics of machine learning and the math behind it. As a data scientist, this was of great interest it me.

Lastly, there is the mentorship program. During their junior year, students are paired up with a mentor (usually an engineer) from a company with an office in the area. The student and mentor meet twice a month to discuss topics such as personal development, career development, projects, and more. My mentor was Gorak Tulla, who is a Solutions Architect for Walmart. When it came to our interactions, there was one thing that immediately stood out to me: the difference in our personalities. Gorak, in a sense, went with the flow, while I was more so constant in work mode (almost paranoid) and had way too much on my plate, which was hurting the quality of my work. Through our many meetings, I definitely figured out more about who I was as a person, as a student, and as an engineer, which translated well into my personal and professional life.

On an overall note, my experience as a student in Dublin High School's Dublin Engineering and Design Academy (Computer Science pathway) was quite enjoyable. I worked on projects throughout my four classes (Computer Science and Engineering, Princples of Engineering, AP Computer Science A, Honors Digital Electronics) that really pushed me to take new concepts I had learned and combine them with familiar concepts to continue inventing and innovating. In that regard, I will say that I was and still am a fan of the annual, year-end entreprenuership project, where students build any sort of computer science and engineering project that solves any sort of problem at any scale. Some of those proejcts became ideas that I could proudly present on my portfolio, and some even became commercial projects or startups that I built out. Either way, the academy was well worth the work, and if I had the chance to go back and change my experience of it, I wouldn't.
